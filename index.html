Debra M. Story
by Olgo Vasilevsky on June 08, 2021
Tell us a little about
yourself or your family
or your hobbies.
a. I have a masters in mathematics with a specialty in statistics; I worked in the computer industry; I've worked in
education as an Academic Dean; I worked at Cape Kennedy during the Apollo Program for three months and the
Skylab Program for three months. My name is on the monuments dedicated to the Spaceworker's Hall of Fame. I
worked with Broadway legend, Ann Reinking, for 15 years. We created world renowned Broadway Theatre Project,
which is a training school for those who wish to perhaps have a career on Broadway. They are taught by the best in
the business from Broadway and Hollywood. In addition to this, I was Ann's Assistant Choreographer on Chicago
and Fosse on Broadway. I worked on all national and international tours. With Fosse, I eventually became the
Director/Choreographer of the International Tour. I was the Associate Choreographer on Look of Love at
Roundabout Theatre and a number of other projects.
b. My father was a mechanical engineer for IBM at Cape Kennedy. He was known as a very valuable member of the
launch team. He found an error in one of the capsules and as a result of bringing it to the attention of all he won the
highest award a space worker can be awarded. His name is on all of the monuments at the Spaceworkers Walk of
Fame. My mother was simply the strongest and most admirable woman to have as one's mother.

If none, what is your position on
how you express yourself through
fashion?
I have enough pieces of odd bits of tops and bottoms that I am able to put together
outfits that show that I am not conservative but most likely dabble in an artistic field.
Do you feel there is a link between our
way to express ourselves via fashion
and women empowerment
I feel that the ability for a woman to choose to dress the way she wishes to for a particular
event indicates empowerment. When there is a business party and all of the women show up
in conservative pants and tops, it shows me that a 'costume' has been assigned and that has
been dictated by their job place or that of their mates. It shows lack of empowerment. I would
wear what I wanted to wear.
What do you personally think
about women empowerment as
a movement and what it means
for you?
I have always had to deal with fighting for power for women. Given that I worked toward a
Masters in Mathematics in the early 70's, my classes were very small and the number of
women never exceeded 3. When I worked at the Cape, my manager once slapped me on the
backside. When I worked in education, my superior once said, I wish I could take you on a slow
boat to China. There was really no one to go to about these things at that time but there was
certainly a way to handle it. You simply cooled your heels when it came to being in the
presence of that person. You cooled your attitude, also, not to the point of rudeness, however. I
tell students I teach that when it comes to the casting couch - LOSE THE JOB! by walking out!
You have the power.
